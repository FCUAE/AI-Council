

# Verdict: SaaS Credit Pricing Restructure

## Decision & Rationale

**Option B (decrease credits charged per prompt) is the correct choice.** All three council members converged on this, and the reasoning is airtight. I'll state the key reasons decisively, then provide the exact numbers.

### Why Option B wins on every dimension:

- **Existing users benefit immediately.** Their current credit balances stretch further. Option A would devalue existing balances — users who already purchased credits get nothing unless you run a complex migration. Claude called this "devastating"; GPT-5.4 Pro flagged the same operational nightmare. This alone is decisive.
- **Visibility at the moment of decision.** Users see "this prompt costs 18 credits instead of 30." The savings are concrete and observable. Option A ("now get 160 credits instead of 100!") requires users to do math to feel the benefit.
- **Implementation is trivial.** Change formula parameters. Deploy. Done. No balance migrations, no dual credit systems, no grandfather clauses.
- **Reversibility.** If margins are too thin, adjust parameters incrementally. You can't claw back credits you've already given.
- **Marketing punch.** "We've reduced prompt costs by up to 35%" beats "We've increased credit pack sizes" every time.

### The deeper problem all members identified:

Simply swapping 0.55 → 0.40 in the formula **will not produce consistent 40% margins**. The variance (57%–91%) is driven by three structural issues:

1. **Cost estimation inaccuracy** — Your Test 4 charged 160 credits, implying an estimated cost of ~$6.38, but actual cost was $2.26. Your cost *tracking* is accurate; your cost *estimation* is wildly off for attachment-heavy prompts.
2. **niceRound always rounds up** — systematically inflating charges above the formula's intent.
3. **The minimum floor (max 3)** — cheap prompts get overcharged enormously.

You must fix estimation accuracy alongside the formula change, or you'll just have margins of 40%–75% instead of 57%–91%.

### The new formula:

```
bufferedCost = estimatedApiCost × 1.05
rawCredits = bufferedCost / ($0.0957 × (1 - 0.40))
finalCredits = max(2, niceRound(rawCredits))
```

Simplified:
```
rawCredits = bufferedCost / $0.05742
```

**Parameter changes:**

| Parameter | Old | New | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|
| Failure buffer | 1.08 (8%) | **1.05 (5%)** | Your data proves cost tracking is 1:1. 5% covers occasional retries comfortably. |
| Target margin | 0.55 | **0.40** | Your stated goal. |
| WORST_CASE_NET | $0.0957 | **$0.0957** | Unchanged — anchored to Visionary pack net of fees. |
| Minimum credits | 3 | **2** | Reduces overcharging on cheap prompts. At 2 credits (Visionary), revenue = $0.198, covering any sub-$0.10 call. |

**Critical caveat on "consistent 40%":** 40% will be your Visionary-tier margin floor. Explorer users pay $0.15/credit vs. Visionary's $0.099/credit, so Explorer margins will be ~55-60% on the same prompts. This is **normal and correct** — bulk buyers get better unit economics. Trying to equalize margins across pack tiers would require charging different credit amounts based on which pack users bought, which is confusing and feels unfair.

### The 4 test cases under new pricing:

Using actual Replit costs and the new formula. I'm assuming estimation accuracy is **fixed** so that estimatedApiCost ≈ actualCost (which is your primary action item).

| Test | Actual Cost | Buffered (×1.05) | Raw Credits | Final Credits (niceRound) | Old Credits | Visionary Margin | Explorer Margin |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 (Premium, first) | $1.27 | $1.334 | 23.2 | **24** | 30 | **47%** | **65%** |
| 2 (Premium, follow-up) | $1.29 | $1.355 | 23.6 | **24** | 40 | **46%** | **64%** |

**Key observations:**
- Visionary margins cluster tightly at 44–47% — much closer to 40% than the old 57–91% range
- The remaining 4–7% overshoot above 40% comes from the 5% buffer and niceRound-up. This is acceptable — it's your safety margin.
- **Test 4 drops from 160 to 42 credits** — a 74% reduction. This proves the old estimation was wildly inflating attachment costs. Fix your estimator.
- **Test 2 drops from 40 to 24** — the old formula was double-charging for context in follow-up prompts.

### On the 8% failure buffer:

**Reduce to 5%.** Your own data confirms Replit charges are 1:1 with OpenRouter. The buffer's purpose is retry coverage, not estimation correction. In a ~7-10 API call pipeline, one retry adds ~1-2% to total cost. Two retries add ~3-4%. Five percent covers this with headroom. If your retry rate exceeds 3-4% of calls, that's a reliability bug, not a pricing problem. **Log retry rates for 2 weeks post-change to validate.**

### Risks and edge cases:

1. **Estimation accuracy is your #1 risk.** The formula change is meaningless if your estimator thinks a $2.26 prompt will cost $6.38. Audit your attachment cost estimation immediately — it appears to be the primary source of overcharging.
2. **niceRound inflation.** Consider tightening rounding rules: round to nearest integer below 10 (not nearest 2), nearest 2 below 20, nearest 5 below 50, nearest 10 above 50. This reduces systematic upward bias.
3. **Sub-$0.10 prompts.** Even with min=2, a $0.03 API call yields 90%+ margin at Visionary rates. Consider min=1 for prompts with estimated cost < $0.05.
4. **Revenue impact.** You're cutting revenue per prompt by ~25-35% (more on overcharged prompts). Ensure your volume can absorb this, or plan for it as a growth investment.
5. **Communicate the change proactively.** Email existing users: "We've reduced prompt costs — your credits now go further." This is goodwill you can bank.

## Dissent Notes

The Gemini response was truncated and could not be fully evaluated, but it raised a valid structural point about the impossibility of pre-calculating output token costs. Claude overstated this as "sloppy reasoning," but the underlying tension is real: estimation will always lag reality. GPT-5.4 Pro correctly identified that Claude's claim about Options A and B being "mathematically equivalent" is wrong — they differ in how they affect per-credit revenue, existing balances, breakage, and minimum charge economics. I agree with GPT-5.4 Pro's critique here, though it doesn't change the recommendation since Option B wins on operational grounds regardless.

The strongest opposing argument would be Claude's suggestion of an **absolute margin floor + percentage margin** (e.g., $0.15 minimum profit + 30% margin). This is theoretically sounder but adds implementation complexity that isn't justified at the user's current scale.

## Conditions for Reversal

Option A would become preferable if: (1) you discover that >30% of your revenue comes from users who buy credits and never fully use them ("breakage revenue"), since Option B reduces breakage value while Option A preserves it; (2) your estimation accuracy cannot be improved and you need pack-level adjustments to absorb variance; or (3) you plan a full rebrand/relaunch where changing pack sizes signals a "new product" rather than a price cut.

## Confidence

**High — directionally correct ~90% of the time.** The Option B recommendation is near-unanimous across the council and dominates on every practical dimension. The specific numbers (5% buffer, min=2) are calibrated estimates that should be validated with 2-4 weeks of production data.

## Actionable Implication

**Do these three things in order:**

1. **Fix your cost estimator first** — especially for attachments and follow-up prompts. Your Test 4 overestimate ($6.38 estimated vs. $2.26 actual) means the formula change alone won't fix margins. Compare estimated vs. actual costs across 50+ prompts to calibrate.
2. **Deploy the new formula** (buffer=1.05, margin=0.40, min=2) and announce it as a user-facing price reduction.
3. **Instrument and monitor** — log retry rates, estimation accuracy, and realized margins per prompt for 2 weeks. Adjust buffer (5% → 6%) or minimum (2 → 1) based on data.
