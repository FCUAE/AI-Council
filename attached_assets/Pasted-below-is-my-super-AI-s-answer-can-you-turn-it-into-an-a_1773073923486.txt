1. PRODUCT POSITIONING & COPY OVERHAUL
1.1 Product Name
The current name "LLM Council" fails because "LLM" is insider jargon that alienates the majority of your target market — knowledge workers, content creators, and decision-makers who think in terms of "AI" or brand names like ChatGPT, not "Large Language Models."

The recommendation is to brand the product publicly as "Council" while retaining "LLM Council" as the parent company or legal entity name. However, the peer reviews correctly identified that "Council" alone is dangerously generic for SEO, trademark protection, and discoverability. A single common English word will never rank against local governments, gaming wikis, and thousands of other entities.

The solution: Use "CouncilAI" as the registrable brand and domain target (councilaI.com, getcouncil.ai, or similar), but market the product conversationally as "Council" in all UI and copy. Think of how "Notion" works — the word is generic but the brand context makes it specific. The logo, the UI, the ".ai" domain all disambiguate.

Name evaluation:

CouncilAI — Best balance of memorability, SEO-friendliness, domain availability, and trademark defensibility. Reads as "Council AI" naturally. Use this for domain, legal, app stores, and SEO. In-product, just say "Council."

The Council — Premium and cinematic but harder to protect legally and impossible to rank for in search. Use "The Council" in marketing copy and taglines but not as the registered product name.

Deliberate AI — Communicates the concept but sounds enterprise-sterile and forgettable.

Verdict — Strong outcome word but doesn't convey the multi-model deliberation that makes the product unique.

Quorum — Too obscure for mainstream adoption.

Final decision: Register and build as CouncilAI. In all product UI, hero copy, and conversation, refer to "Council" or "the Council." Footer reads "Council is a product of CouncilAI." Internal codebase remains llm-council.

1.3 Three-Step Process Naming
The current names — Hearing, Review, Verdict — mix courtroom metaphor (Hearing, Verdict) with academic language (Review). The peer reviews correctly identified this inconsistency. Pick one metaphor and commit.

Go full courtroom. It is dramatic, authoritative, and maps perfectly to what actually happens:

Step 1: The Hearing. Each AI model independently analyzes your question and presents its case. User-facing description: "Three expert models each draft an independent answer."

Step 2: Cross-Examination. Models challenge and critique each other's reasoning, find gaps, and identify errors. User-facing description: "They critique, fact-check, and stress-test each other's answers."

Step 3: The Verdict. The Chairman synthesizes all arguments and critiques into one definitive answer. User-facing description: "A chairman model synthesizes the strongest elements into one final answer."

"Cross-Examination" replaces the generic "Review" and is the key differentiator. It is the step that makes Council different from every ChatGPT wrapper. The name should convey that drama and rigor.

For progress UI during deliberation, show contextual updates with time expectations:

"Step 1 of 3: The Hearing — Each model is drafting an independent answer (~ 1-3 mins)"
"Step 2 of 3: Cross-Examination — Models are challenging each other's reasoning (~ 1-3 mins)"
"Step 3 of 3: The Verdict — The Chairman is synthesizing the final answer (~ 1-2 mins)"

This addresses the peer review concern about the 60-90 second wait time. Users need to know what is happening and roughly how long it will take, or they will abandon.

1.4 Evidence Vault Renaming
"Evidence Vault" has two problems. "Vault" implies something locked away and hard to access — the opposite of the transparency we want to promote. "Evidence" is clinical and suggests forensics.

The recommendation is "Behind the Verdict" as the primary label. It generates curiosity — people want to peek behind the curtain. It stays in the courtroom metaphor. It implies depth without being intimidating. As a collapsible section toggle, "View Behind the Verdict" / "Hide Behind the Verdict" is natural and inviting.

The peer review suggestion of "Deliberation Log" is accurate but flat and developer-sounding. "The Record" is clean but vague. "Behind the Verdict" wins on intrigue and click-through potential.

Sub-tabs within Behind the Verdict:

Current "Council Thoughts" becomes "Individual Opinions" — Each model's independent answer.
Current "Debate Notes" becomes "Cross-Examination" — Peer critiques and challenges.
Current "Rankings" becomes "Performance Rankings" — Automated scoring with confidence percentages.

1.5 The Chairman Label
"Chairman" is gendered and sounds stuffy for a modern product. The peer reviews flagged this correctly. Replace with "The Synthesizer" in technical contexts and "Chairman" only where the courtroom metaphor demands it for flavor. In the UI, the synthesis result card should be labeled "The Verdict" — users care about the output, not the role that produced it. When you need to reference the model doing the synthesis, call it "the lead model" or "the synthesizing model." In model selection UI, label the chip "Lead Model" rather than "Chairman."

However, in marketing copy and the How It Works section, "Chairman" actually works because it is vivid and evocative — "a Chairman synthesizes the final answer" paints a clear picture. Use it selectively in copy, avoid it in UI labels.

2. LANDING PAGE DESIGN & CONVERSION ARCHITECTURE
2.1 Core Conversion Philosophy
The landing page has one job: get the user to type a prompt. Everything else — copy, design, social proof, pricing — exists to build enough trust and curiosity that the user takes that action. We use a prompt-first funnel where the user experiences the product before signing up. This is the pattern that drove ChatGPT's explosive growth: zero friction to first value.

The critical insight from the peer reviews: the prompt-first auth flow is the single highest-risk UX element. If the user's prompt is lost during the authentication redirect, they are gone forever. This requires triple redundancy and aggressive testing.

2.2 The Prompt-First Conversion Funnel (Exact Flow)
Step 1: User lands on the landing page. They see a hero with a large prompt input above the fold.

Step 2: User types a question and clicks "Ask the Council."

Step 3: System checks authentication status. If not authenticated:

-
Immediately save the prompt text to localStorage under the key "councilpendingprompt"
-
Also encode the prompt as a URL parameter (base64 encoded to handle special characters) and include it in the Clerk redirect URL as a fallback
-
Display a modal overlay (not a full redirect — modals have lower abandonment than redirects): "Create a free account to get your verdict." with Google sign-in button (primary, largest), email sign-up option, and a "Sign in" link for returning users
-
The Clerk sign-up component is rendered inside this modal, styled to match the theme
Step 4: User authenticates via Clerk (Google OAuth is fastest — prioritize this).

Step 5: Clerk redirects to /app?autostart=1. The app page's useEffect hook checks:

-
First: localStorage for "councilpendingprompt"
-
Fallback: URL parameter for the encoded prompt
-
If found: auto-populate the input, auto-submit, create conversation, begin deliberation
-
Clear localStorage and URL params after successful submission
Step 6: User watches their first deliberation unfold. This is the magic moment. The stepped progress (Hearing, Cross-Examination, Verdict) with model-specific status updates makes the wait feel purposeful.

Step 7: User sees their Verdict. They are impressed.

Step 8: Free tier allows 3 total deliberations (lifetime, not monthly — these are expensive). Behind the Verdict is available on free tier because it demonstrates depth and creates desire for more. Model customization is locked to the Starter Council on free tier (cheap LLM models are only offered in the free tier).

Step 9: On the 4th prompt submission, hard paywall: "You've used your free debate. Upgrade to Member for up to 50 debates per month." with pricing cards and one-click Stripe Checkout.

Why 3 free deliberations, not 1: One deliberation lets the user see the output but doesn't build a habit or let them test different question types. Three deliberations create enough investment that the sunk cost of switching away feels real. But 3 is the maximum we can afford — at roughly $0.08-0.15 per deliberation on the Starter Council (cheap models), that is $0.24-0.45 per free user. Acceptable customer acquisition cost.

2.3 Above-the-Fold Strategy
The first viewport must accomplish three things in under 5 seconds: communicate what this product does, make the user believe it works, and present an input field they want to type into.

Layout (desktop):

Top navigation bar: Council logo on the left. "Pricing" and "Examples" links in the center. "Sign in" text link and "Get started free" button on the right. Clean, minimal, dark background.

Hero section occupying the remaining viewport height. Centered layout.

Large H1: "Ask once. Get the best answer — after the top AI models debate it."

Subtitle beneath: "Three elite AI models draft solutions, critique each other, then a lead model synthesizes one clear answer."

Large, prominent prompt input area: Auto-expanding textarea with a visible "Ask the Council" submit button. The input should have a subtle glow or border animation to draw the eye. It should be the largest interactive element on the page.
